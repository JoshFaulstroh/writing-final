A Rhetorical Analysis of Burger King's "Moldy Whopper" Ad Campaign

Joshua Faulstroh


Rhetorical Devices: What are They?

Rhetorical devices are a category of tools that authors and other media designers use to deliver a specific message to a target audience. In some cases, those tools may result in a very successful message delivery and, in other cases, those tools may also lead to the deterring or disinterest of an audience. Anti-advertising is an advertising approach in which companies often use contradictory or oxymoronic content in an ad in order to exploit the effects of reverse psychology on consumers. The companies that decide to take this marketing approach commonly use distasteful, satirical, or delf-deprecating elements not only in an effort to increase sales, but also to take advantage of a psychological effect known as reactance. An example of psychological reactance is when an individual feels the urge to do something immediately after being told not to. Scientifically, it is the brain’s reaction to a apparent threat to an individual’s freedom of choice.

The "Moldy Whopper" Ad Campaign

A particular Burger King advertisement campaign, first aired in 2020, is an example of anti-advertising, where it uses distasteful imagery as well as blatant scientific points to prove the main idea of the advertisement. During the 45-second clip, a timelapse of a molding whopper can be seen, eventually ending on a screen which displays the words: “The beauty of no artificial preservatives.”

Why Display Literal Garbage?

Determining the absolute main purpose of the advertisement can depend on the individual who is watching and analyzing the ad. However, it can be concluded that there are three main reasons as to why Burger King would show rotten product to an audience. The first reason being to prove to the audience that BK does not use artificial preservatives of any kind. The second being to persuade consumers that BK is a potentially healthier alternative compared to other fast food chains. The third and final being to take a stab at other fast food competitors. After doing extensive research on this ad campaign, there are also some additional conclusions that can be drawn. While there are no artificial preservatives in Burger King’s food anymore, that does not necessarily mean that there are no artificial ingredients whatsoever. Additionally, the claim that there are no artificial preservatives in BK’s food can also be seen as a facade to sway the decisions of those who are more focused on what foods lack rather than what they contain.

What are Artificial Preservatives?

Artificial preservatives are synthetic chemicals added to food products to increase shelf life. They are used in numerous ways by a multitude of businesses. Commercial food manufacturers use them to preserve the shelf life of many foods, just as fast food chains use preservatives to prevent spoilage and maintain an overall satisfactory appearance of their food for customers to see. These artificial ingredients that fast food companies use in their food can affect health in several ways.

Burger King's Use & Ban of Artificial Preservatives

Burger King, until February of 2020, used some artificial ingredients such as sodium benzoate, ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid (EDTA), and calcium propionate which, after the ad campaign and Burger King’s commitment to do away with artificial preservatives, were replaced with lactic acid, nothing, and cultured wheat flour respectively. The lack of preservatives in a whopper, that would otherwise be prone to molding after a generally short period of time, would make it easier for mold to grow and thrive on it. This is how Burger king made an effort to prove their food was free of artificial preservatives, where they effectively turned these scientific facts into a source of credibility in a society where an increasing number of people are looking for safer an more natural ingredients. The simple intended purpose of the moldy whopper proving that there are less harmful ingredients than there used to be is a way for Burger King to grasp the more favorable side of the ethos surrounding the controversy of potentially harmful synthetic ingredients in commercial foods. On the note that Burger King’s removal of artificial ingredients in their food can make it relatively safer to eat, it should also be considered that the replacements of those ingredients are very similar to their artificial counterparts. For example, calcium propionate, which is the chemical that was replaced by cultured wheat flour in BK’s burger buns, is the salt of the propionic acid that the bacteria in the cultured flour naturally produce. This replacement makes little difference in terms of functionality, as both ingredients act almost identically in food. The only difference being, one is natural and the other is not. A similar instance can be observed in the meat industry, where celery salt, which includes naturally occurring chemicals known as nitrates that act as a natural preservative, replaces artificially added nitrates. This allows the company to claim that there is no added amount of a particular ingredient, except that which is naturally occurring, such as the chemical produced by the bacteria in BK’s buns. While it is true that Burger King has removed all artificial ingredients, or at least the 120 it has banned, it is also true that the ingredients it has replaced those artificial additives with are nearly identical in terms of function. The list of Burger King’s blacklisted ingredients can be seen as a limiter on what the company can use in its food.

Why Remove Artificial Ingredients?

The removal of artificial ingredients can either remove the risk of harm from consuming a particular food with particular ingredients, or it can allow a company to slap a healthy label on the item they removed synthetic ingredients from. Burger King could have very well had the latter in mind when formulating the Moldy Whopper campaign. In BK’s case, removing the artificial stuff allows them to market their food products in a way that makes them seem drastically healthier. The brief words associated with the campaign, “The Beauty of No Artificial Preservatives,” sort of emphasizes the idea that Burger king’s food is now healthier than ever. In those six words, there are two main selling points. The first being the fact that there are no artificial preservatives and the second being the redefining of beauty as the reality that stems from the first. At the end of the day, however, they are marketing towards those who may be looking for healthier alternatives when it comes to fast food, such as those on lunch break and want a convenient snack or meal.

Word Bias: A Helful Tool in Marketing

In presenting such few words in the advertisements, BK also took advantage of wording bias. Wording bias is seen virtually everywhere in present times. Even products at stores and survey questions take advantage of wording bias to get people to buy a specific product or respond a certain way. For example, the percentage of fat in milk. Some gallons of milk may display that they are 98% fat free, while others display that they have 2% fat. Many may have heard of the situation where a glass can either be seen as half full, rather than half empty. “The beauty of no artificial preservatives” is the reality that there are nearly identical ingredients included; however, the sentence that BK used can appeal to those who care more about what a food lacks, rather than what it contains.

Graphics & Music

Some additional methods that BK used to deliver their message were through the graphics and music of the advertisement. In terms of graphics, they displayed their flagship product against a black background, which places all focus on what is on the whopper instead of background distractions, which are avoided effectively by a solid black background that offers more contrast against the bright colors of the burger. Also, the song used in the original ad resonated with the idea of the timelapse from the fact that the song title is “What Difference a Day Makes” by Dinah Washington while the timelapse shows the day-by-day changes of the moldy burger.

Competition

The third reason why BK chose to display a moldy Whopper was to take a stab at other fast food chain competitors who may have not been brave enough to show their own moldy food to their own customers. A sense of disgusted admiration may be evoked because of this, which is why some members of the audience may be swayed into buying Burger King’s product. It is not a common occurrence when a company tries to increase sales by showing its consumers literal garbage so, when a certain part of an audience sees this brave effort made, it may spark a sense of interest, not only because of the fact that a company is displaying rotten food, but the message behind the rotten food. Other competitors to BK, namely McDonalds, have been known to have food items that sat in customers’ cars and houses for months, years even, without rotting or showing any signs of decomposition whatsoever. The viewers of the moldy whopper ads that know this fact may be even more swayed, in the fact that Burger King is proving that their food products are actually prone to the natural environment compared to the instances where McDonalds’ quarter-pounders were as effective at keeping away mold as great as copper doorknobs are at keeping away germs.

In Conclusion

Taking a stab at competitors is a tactic companies use to get the general public to buy their product, as well as other methods such as tweaking ingredient lists to make products seem like a healthier alternative as well as outright proving that the company has done away with ingredients that are generally shunned by many people. Burger King has achieved these three things in a 45-second clip of their famous Whopper growing mold over a span of 34 days. A lack of artificial preservatives, and ingredients entirely, can legitimately make a food item healthier just as effectively as it can act as a mask to cover up naturally identical ingredients to artificial counterparts. Additionally, when a company compares oneself to others as Burger king did, when it compared the naturalness of its food composition to other fast food companies’, it could prove effective as a marketing tactic to persuade consumers into buying for certain reasons, such as concerns about what goes into food. In a world where people are steadily growing more concerned about how natural the ingredients are in their food, Burger King has taken advantage of these concerns to strategically market in a way that makes it seem like a very healthy alternative in a generally unhealthy industry.
